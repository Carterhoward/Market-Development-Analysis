04

What does not move the needle

Several metrics that seem intuitively important show no statistically significant correlation with retention in this dataset. Acting on these without supporting evidence would be misallocating resources.

Counter-intuitive finding

Project hunger does not significantly predict retention

Hartford retains at 81% with 35x hunger. Milwaukee retains at 74% with 69x hunger. High scarcity does not automatically mean low retention. The relationship exists but is not statistically significant across 75 markets — meaning other factors dominate. Reducing hunger alone is not a reliable lever.

Counter-intuitive finding

Active GC count % does not predict retention

Simply having more GCs registered and active has no meaningful correlation with whether subs renew. What matters is whether those GCs are posting projects — a step further down the activation chain. GC acquisition without GC activation is not moving retention.

Counter-intuitive finding

Sub-to-GC ratio does not predict retention

The ratio of active subs to active GCs shows no significant correlation with retention (r=−0.11, not significant). The absolute supply-demand balance is less important than whether the GCs who are active are actually generating projects for subs to bid on.

05

Six findings that require action

01 — Critical

Project penetration is the most under-tracked metric in the portfolio

Only 6.5% of commercial permits become posted projects on PH on average. Markets above 10% penetration retain at 72.6% vs 67.8% below 6% — a 4.8pp gap driven entirely by GC project posting behavior. This metric is not currently on the retention dashboard but has the clearest causal connection to renewal. 26 of 75 markets are below 5%.

02 — Needs investigation

Cincinnati's hunger metric (46x) requires a manual data pull

At 46 subs per commercial project, Cincinnati is more than twice the next highest priority market — yet it retains at 71%. This is either a data capture issue (commercial project count may be understated), or Cincinnati subs are renewing for reasons entirely unrelated to project matching. Either scenario needs resolution before Cincinnati data is used in any analysis.

03 — Warning

GC activation — not GC acquisition — is the actual bottleneck

GCs are registering and becoming "active" without posting projects at the rate needed to sustain subs. GCs posting recently (the 90-day window) is a significant retention predictor; active GC count is not. The investment should shift from getting GCs on the platform to getting GCs to post from their permit pipeline.

04 — Structural

"Healthy" can now be formally defined

The five healthy markets share a consistent profile: hunger ≤ 18x, active paid sub % ≥ 38%, project penetration ≥ 7%, and GCs posting rate ≥ 19%. This cluster is consistent enough to formalize as a benchmark. Any priority market hitting all four thresholds should graduate to "healthy" status. Markets missing two or more are at retention risk.

05 — Warning

San Francisco's 75x project hunger is the most extreme in the portfolio

SF has 75 subs searching for every 1 commercial project — the highest of any market, priority or otherwise. Combined with 64% retention (declining) and only 11% GC posting rate, SF is the market most urgently in need of supply-side intervention. Without GC activation, sub acquisition in SF accelerates churn, not revenue.

06 — Opportunity

Hartford and Milwaukee break the hunger-retention model — understand why

Hartford retains at 81% with 35x hunger. Milwaukee at 74% with 69x hunger. Both significantly outperform their project scarcity levels. Something in those markets is creating renewal value that isn't captured by the marketplace metrics in this sheet. Identifying it — whether product features, local sales relationships, or market composition — would give a replicable playbook for supply-constrained markets.
