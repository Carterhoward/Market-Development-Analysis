05

Markets to watch

Approaching healthy — Austin & Nashville

Two new "Next?" markets flagged in the 3-month view

Austin retains at 76% with hunger of just 12x and GC posting at 18% — the profile of a market on the verge of healthy designation. Nashville holds steady at 72% with GC posting now at 20%, clearing the threshold. Both were not flagged in the March snapshot, suggesting the 3-month average is picking up improving momentum that a single month missed.

Declining trend — Cleveland & Sacramento

Two priority markets show −2 to −3pp when smoothed

Cleveland drops from 70% (Mar) to 67% (3mo avg) — a 3pp gap that suggests March was an unusually good month, not representative of the trend. Sacramento similarly drops from 71% to 69%, and its hunger rises from 16x to 31x on the smoothed view. Both markets warrant closer monitoring.

Persistent underperformer — San Francisco

SF hunger (37x in 3mo) and GC posting (6%) are the worst in the portfolio

The 3-month average actually worsens SF's picture on hunger (75x Mar → 37x smoothed, still the highest among priority markets) while confirming GC posting at just 6% — the lowest of any tracked market. With 64% retention in March falling to 62% in the 3mo view, SF has no improving momentum. Supply-side intervention is overdue.

Confirmed healthy — Atlanta improved

Atlanta's 3pp retention gain in the 3mo view validates its healthy status

Atlanta moves from 69% (March) to 72% (3mo avg) — the largest positive delta of any market. Its GC posting rate of 25% is the highest among healthy markets. The smoothed view confirms this isn't a March anomaly: Atlanta is genuinely improving and represents a replicable model for other Southeast markets.

06

Six findings — confirmed or updated

01 — Confirmed & strengthened

GC posting rate is the single most actionable leading indicator

In the March snapshot, GCs posting recently had a moderate correlation (r=+0.25). In the 3-month average, it strengthens to r=+0.47 — nearly as strong as active paid sub %. This is the metric most under your direct control: GC activation campaigns, posting incentives, and permit-to-project workflows all move this number. Every healthy market posts above 19%. No market posting below 15% retains above 70%.

02 — Now confirmed

High project hunger does hurt retention — the 3-month data makes it statistically clear

In March, hunger showed a negative trend but wasn't statistically significant (r=−0.18, n.s.). Smoothed over 3 months, it strengthens to r=−0.36 with p<0.01. The relationship is real: markets with too many subs competing for too few projects do churn more. The threshold appears to be around 18–20x — above that, retention pressure builds.

03 — Updated

Cincinnati's 46x hunger in March was likely a data anomaly

The 3-month average brings Cincinnati's hunger to 28x — still above the healthy benchmark of ≤18x, but no longer an extreme outlier. The March spike was most likely a single month with abnormally low commercial project postings. Cincinnati's 71% retention is consistent across both views, which confirms it's not in crisis — but the supply gap at 28x still warrants attention.

04 — New finding from 3mo view

Bids per project is now a significant retention predictor

In March, bids per project showed no significant correlation with retention (r=+0.22, n.s.). In the 3-month view, it strengthens to r=+0.32 with p<0.01. This suggests that markets where subs are actively bidding — not just searching — are the ones that renew. Bid submission rate may be a better proxy for "getting value" than simply being active on the platform.

05 — Actionable

"Healthy" can be formally defined — and two markets are knocking on the door

Healthy markets consistently share: hunger ≤ 18x, GC posting ≥ 19–20%, active paid sub % ≥ 38%, and project penetration ≥ 7%. The 3-month view adds Austin and Nashville as "Next?" — both hit 3 of 4 thresholds. A formal graduation criteria using these four metrics would give the team a clear target for each priority market.

06 — Open question

The lag between KPI improvement and retention is still unmeasured

Both views are cross-sectional snapshots — they show correlation but not lead time. The monthly time series data exists in the full Google Sheet and would allow quantifying how many months after GC posting rises above 19% we should expect to see retention move. That panel analysis would convert these directional signals into a predictive model. Recommend as next step.
