Critical distinction: GC posting RATE vs GC project COUNT

Markets with more total GC projects posted (raw volume) don't retain better — in fact, the relationship is slightly negative. But markets where a higher share of active GCs are regularly posting do retain better. The distinction is activation rate — getting GCs who are already registered to post consistently — not raw volume or new GC acquisition.

05

Six findings from the full analysis

01 — Primary finding

GC posting rate is the only metric that genuinely leads retention — and the signal strengthens over time

At a 6-month lag, GC posting rate (r=+0.33, p<0.001) is the strongest predictor in the dataset. Critically, the correlation strengthens as the lag increases — from r=+0.30 at 1 month to r=+0.33 at 6 months. This confirms it's not coincident with retention: it precedes it. When GC posting rate rises in a market, expect retention to improve ~6 months later. Sustained markets average 19.0% posting rate vs 14.4% for others — a 4.6pp structural gap.

02 — Most concerning finding

Many previously strong markets are in deep decline — the streak has ended

Kansas City peaked at 92% retention in early 2024 and is now at 66% (−26pp). Richmond went from 89% to 67% (−22pp). San Jose from 85% to 54% (−31pp). Virginia Beach from 89% to 71% (−18pp). These markets sustained above 75% for 13–18 consecutive months, then broke. Kansas City showed all three verified KPIs declining before its break — the clearest pre-collapse pattern in the dataset. However, Richmond, Virginia Beach, and Milwaukee all had rising or stable GC posting rates before their collapses, meaning GC posting rate alone is not a reliable collapse predictor. Active sub % declining in the 3-month window is the most consistent warning signal (present in 53% of collapses). Some breaks — including Milwaukee's 28pp drop — showed no warning from any verified metric.

03 — Actionable finding

Pittsburgh, Louisville, and Austin are the three markets to study for a replicable playbook

These are the only three markets currently above 75% AND trending upward. Pittsburgh recovered from a 2024 trough (66% → 76%, +10pp). Louisville showed a V-shaped recovery (70% → 79%). Austin dipped and recovered (81% → 67% trough → 78% current). In all three, GC posting rates are at or above the 18–19% threshold. Understanding what changed in these markets 6 months before their retention improvement is the highest-value remaining analysis.

04 — Important distinction

GC project volume doesn't predict retention — GC posting rate does

Raw GC project count shows no significant correlation with retention at any lag (r=−0.05 to −0.07). This means adding more GC projects to a market doesn't automatically improve retention. What matters is whether the GCs who are active are posting consistently. The mechanism is reliability of supply — subs need to see regular project activity to stay engaged, not just a high volume in any single month.

05 — Strategic concern

Detroit is the most structurally healthy market — and it's been that way for 26 months

Detroit has sustained above 75% for 26 consecutive months (all but January 2024), averaging 79% with a +6pp trend. Its GC posting rate has been consistently above 20% since mid-2024. It's the only market with a streak running essentially the full 27-month window. Understanding what makes Detroit different — GC mix, commercial density, market maturity, or product usage patterns — would provide the clearest benchmark for what a fully healthy market looks like.

06 — Open question

Project penetration is structural, not a short-term lever

Project penetration % (% of commercial permits that become posted projects on PH) has a consistent but weakening signal across lags (r=+0.20 at 1 month, +0.18 at 6 months). Unlike GC posting rate, the signal doesn't strengthen with lag — meaning it's a baseline condition, not a leading indicator. Improving it requires changing GC behavior around permit-to-posting conversion, which is a slower structural change than activating GCs who are already registered. It should be tracked as a health metric, not as a leading indicator.
